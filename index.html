Prologue

Disaffection

By the 1880s, the Indigenous peoples of the Plains in the West were facing a disaster. These groups included the Cree, Siksika, Kainai, Piikani, and Saulteaux. The buffalo disappeared entirely from the plains in Canada. They had once flourished in the millions in the early 19th century. The Indigenous peoples of the Plains began to starve, as the buffalo was their primary food. This disappearance of the buffalo wasn't natural. The lucrative robe trade encouraged white settlers and Indigenous peoples to overhunt the plains bison. Additionally, westward migration destroyed grazing grounds and introduced new diseases.

Bison Skulls (1892)

A pile of buffalo skulls awaiting indstrial processing

After Canada acquired the Hudson's Bay Company's territory (Rupert's Land), many Indigenous groups signed treaties to prevent starvation. In return for reserve lands, money, and protection, their lands were ceded to Canada. These treaties ended their traditional nomadic way of life, forcing them to turn to agriculture instead. The Indigenous peoples watched as farms, railways, and towns appeared on the prairies in the late 19th century.

The treaties did not solve the Native peoples' problems. In the winter of 1884, an estimated one-tenth of the plains Indigenous peoples died from starvation, malnutrition, and disease. The now-scarce buffalo had provided their food, clothing, and shelter. Eventually, they became dependent on government rations. The government would sometimes cut rations to twist them into signing treaties. In 1880, the chiefs Big Bear and Crowfoot formed a confederacy to solve these problems.

On June 16, 1884, the Cree chief Mistahimaskwa (Big Bear) hosted a gathering. More than 2000 Cree people attended. After a young warrior named Kawicitwemot assaulted a farmer for denying him food, the North-West Mounted Police (NWMP) arrived. 400 Cree warriors met the police. Mistahimaskwa managed to defuse the situation, allowing the police to apprehend Kawicitwemot peacefully. This incident compelled the Canadian government to send more police to the West, fearing a conflict.

Meanwhile, the Métis had their grievances. After the Red River Rebellion in 1870, many settled in the Fort Carlton region, founding several settlements on the South Saskatchewan River. Their lives as fur traders were impacted by the near-extinction of the buffalo, so they were dependent on these lands for sustenance. In 1883, the government sold one of their villages to the Prince Albert Colonization Company. The Métis believed that the government was not respecting their land, farms, and homesteads. Furthermore, many of them felt that the government was not protecting their rights and identity.

Section 1

Insurgency

On March 24, 1884, a group of Métis in Batoche decided that Louis Riel should return from exile and represent their cause. On May 6, in a meeting attended by the Métis and sympathetic white representatives from Prince Albert, they decided to send a delegation to ask Riel to return. Riel arrived in Manitoba on July 4. He encouraged the people of the North-West to unite and press their case on the federal government, and in Ottawa, the Conservative government began to fear a second rebellion.

In December 1884, Riel drafted a petition to the government. In return, the government offered little more than an investigation into Métis land claims. Unhappy with the government's response, the Métis set up a provisional government on March 18 and seized the parish church at Batoche, demanding the surrender of the nearby Fort Carlton. Riel became the president, while hunter and tactician Gabriel Dumont became their military commander.

Revolutionary Bill of Rights (1885)

The Regina Leader on April 21, 1885, containing Riel's "Revolutionary Bill of Rights"

On March 25, 1885, Dumont brought 30 men to gather supplies from a store near the community of Duck Lake. Meanwhile, the superintendent of the NWMP, Leif Crozier, received word that the Métis were planning to occupy Duck Lake, unaware that this had already happened. He sent fifteen police and seven armed volunteers to get provisions from the store. Dumont and his warriors intercepted the police, and the Canadians returned to Fort Carlton, not wanting to risk a physical engagement. Crozier returned with a force of fifty-six police and forty-three volunteers to take supplies from the store.

In the early morning of March 26, Dumont set up an ambush outside Duck Lake, but some of Crozier's scouts were aware of his plan. Crozier's men prepared a mounted barricade, and the Métis formed a semi-circle to attack the police on both flanks. Dumont sent his brother Isidore and a chief named Assiwiyin with a white flag to distract Crozier's forces, but the police shot both of them. The Métis began to fire from all sides. The police retreated into a clearing, where the Métis ambushed them. Dumont was wounded by a bullet but stayed to motivate his men. Within half an hour, Crozier ordered a retreat. The police fled, and although Dumont's brother Édouard wanted to pursue them, Riel ordered a stop to the bloodshed. The Métis had five casualties while the police had twelve. Colonel Irvine, Crozier's superior, decided to evacuate and destroy Fort Carlton on March 28, and they moved to Prince Albert.

Battle of Duck Lake

An illustration of the Battle of Duck Lake

After the Métis victory, sympathetic Cree bands raided stores and farms in Battleford, Saskatchewan, led by the chiefs Big Bear and Poundmaker. On March 28, the residents of Battleford fled to the nearby NWMP post for safety. The Cree stole horses and cattle then killed two farmers. On March 30, the bands took food and supplies from the abandoned stores and houses then returned to Poundmaker's reserve with the loot. They continued to plunder the town until April 24, when General Sir William Dillon Otter's arrived. They had burned most of the town's public buildings and houses.

Battleford Beleagued

A newspaper article about the Looting of Battleford

During the Cree uprising, young Cree men led by the war chief Wandering Spirit attacked officials, clergy, and settlers in Frog Lake, a small settlement in present-day Alberta. In the early morning of April 2, they took federal Indian agent Thomas Quinn and several other white settlers hostage. The Cree ordered the prisoners to go to their encampment. When Quinn refused, they shot him in the head. Despite Big Bear's attempts to stop the massacre, the band killed another eight unarmed settlers, including two priests. Seventy others from the town were taken captive. One of the men, a clerk named William Bleasdell Cameron, escaped with the help of sympathetic Cree, who sheltered him at their camp. Then, they burned the church. This event became known as the Frog Lake Massacre.

On April 15, 200 Cree warriors attacked Fort Pitt. They intercepted a police party, killing a constable and wounding another. The garrison commander Francis Dickens (son of Charles Dickens) surrendered. Big Bear released the police officers but kept the townspeople hostage. Then, the Cree destroyed the fort.

Section 2

Defeat

The government's reaction was decisive. On March 25, 1885, militia mobilization began, even though there were only a few hundred full-time Canadian soldiers. 300 Canadian troops were transported across the unfinished gaps of the Canadian Pacific Railway, and they arrived at Qu'Appelle, Saskatchewan, on April 10. Most were Ontarians, but there were also three battalions from Quebec and Nova Scotia. There were an additional 1700 troops from the West, totaling around 5000 under the command of General Frederick Middleton.

Middleton's troops left Qu'Appelle on April 10 and advanced upstream along the South Saskatchewan River. The scouts discovered an apparent ambush by Dumont's Métis and Dakota force at Fish Creek. The Métis decided to attack Middleton's forces at Tourond's Coulée, a nearby ravine. Dumont brought 200 fighters with him to the site. Meanwhile, Middleton set up a camp on a farm 10 kilometers south of Fish Creek. The next day, as Middleton's militia attempted to cross the ravine, a scout noticed the tracks of an inexperienced Métis soldier and followed them. The scout approached Dumont and his men, but before he could catch the scout, the Métis and Canadians had met, and the ambush began. Dumont shot the scout and returned to the rifle pits.

General Sir Frederick Dobson Middleton

A photograph of General Middleton, the commander of the Militia of Canada

After the ambush, the Métis withdrew into cover and switched to sniping to conserve ammunition. Half of Middleton's force was on the other side of the river, and his artillery fire on the Métis had little effect, although it drove their Cree allies away. Many Métis soldiers wanted to flee, as they were vastly outnumbered, but Dumont convinced them to stay. The Canadians weren't making much progress, and with casualties mounting, they undertook several rushes into the ravine, which only succeeded in causing more deaths. When the sun began to set, Middleton opted for retreat. Although outnumbered, the Métis only suffered eleven casualties.

On April 13, a column led by William Dillon Otter split from Middleton's force to save Battleford from the Cree. They arrived on April 24 and found hundreds of civilians crammed into the fort, but not any of Poundmaker's men. Pressured by the civilians and his troops, Otter decided to take action. He received permission to "punish Poundmaker" and set out with cavalry, militia, volunteers, and two field rifles towards Cut Knife Hill, where 1500 Cree and Assiniboine men, women, and children were.

Military Map of the Cut Knife Battlefield

A map of the battlefield of the Battle of Cut Knife drawn in 1886

On May 2, just after sunrise, Otter's column arrived. To reach the camp, the men crossed the creek and waded through the marsh. An old Cree man awoke when he heard the sound of soldiers and alerted the others. Otter's cannons destroyed the camp's lodges, and the women and children ran towards the ravines for safety. Assiniboine warriors charged at Otter's men to defend the women and children. The Canadians formed a wedge, with two lines of soldiers and police facing the ravines while the volunteers and militia guarded the rear, facing the marsh. The Cree employed a flanking maneuver, stealthily moving towards the soldiers. They almost encircled Otter's men. Finally, the Canadians chose to withdraw through the marsh. As they retreated, some of the Cree fighters mounted their horses. Poundmaker convinced his men to let them leave.

The Battle of Cut Knife

A sketch of the Battle of Cut Knife

On May 7, Middleton reached Gabriel's Crossing and advanced to within 13 kilometers of Batoche. Middleton planned to encircle the Métis by unloading troops at the rear of the town by a steamboat named Northcote. However, the steamboat arrived before the planned attack, and the Métis sliced off its masts and smokestacks by lowering the ferry cable. On May 9, Middleton approached the church at Mission Ridge, unaware that the steamboat was damaged. After finding only priests, nuns, women, and children, they advanced to shell the town.

The Steamboat Northcote

The damaged steamboat Northcote after the battle

The militia advanced about half a kilometer before heavy fire on both sides forced them to take cover. The Métis snipers hid in inconspicuous rifle pits, and the Canadians could not spot them. The Métis then attacked the Canadian artillery, but they fired at the attackers. The Métis then attempted to outflank the militia, resulting in heavy fighting. In the afternoon, the artillery bombarded the rifle pits to no avail. Towards the end of the first day, Middleton ordered the Canadians to retire into temporary fortifications behind a network of improvised barricades for the night.

Canadian Soldiers Preparing to Sleep

Middleton's men preparing to sleep in the makeshift fort on May 9

On May 10 and 11, Middleton made heavily defended gun-pits and shelled the town. The Métis' heavy fire prevented attempted Canadian advancements. To test the enemy's strength, Middleton dispatched a group of men north. At the same time, he conducted an assault at the front. The Métis redirected some of their forces to hold off the northward flank and did not have the necessary power to oppose the central assault. The Canadians were able to make it as far as the Batoche cemetery before turning back. Middleton, satisfied with the result, planned to assault the town the next morning using this method.

Military Map of Batoche

A sketch of the battlefield of the Battle of Batoche

By May 12, the Métis were running out of ammunition. They resorted to using rocks as ammunition or hunting for bullets fired by the Canadians. Three-quarters were wounded. Meanwhile, Middleton enacted his plan, leading his group north to distract the Métis while Colonel van Straubenzee led the main assault. However, van Straubenzee failed to hear the gunfire from the north and did not attack.

After the failed assault, the senior Canadian officers, angry with Middleton's caution, decided to rush straight at the Métis' rifle pits. Many Métis soldiers were too far to help because of Middleton's diversion, and those who remained did not have enough ammunition, but they resisted bravely. The charging Canadian militia stormed into Batoche as the diverted Indigenous fighters returned. They fired at the Canadians from rifle pits near the village. However, the Canadian charge was too overwhelming, and the last defenders of Batoche surrendered. Straubenzee's soldiers drove out the remaining Métis from the town.

Section 3

Prosecution

Although the Battle of Batoche had virtually ended the Rebellion, some small battles occurred after. The first was the Battle of Frenchman's Butte. On May 14, a group of men led by General Strange left Edmonton and caught up to the Frog Lake Cree at Frenchman's Butte. The Cree dug in at the top of a steep hill. Directly advancing towards the Cree would have been difficult, so instead, they opted to fire from long range for several hours. Eventually, both sides retreated. The second was the Battle of Loon Lake. On June 3, a group of police and fighters led by Major Sam Steele attacked a group of Plains Cree warriors and saved a group of hostages. The Cree suffered 5-12 casualties, and the Canadians captured Wandering Spirit.

On May 15, a group of scouts spotted Riel and approached him. After a brief conversation, Riel declared that he was giving himself up. After a brief stay in Middleton's camp near Batoche, he was sent to a prison in Regina to await trial. He was charged with six counts of high treason, which carried a mandatory death sentence.

Riel as a Prisoner at Batoche

A photograph of Louis Riel in the camp of Middleton at Batoche

The prosecutors at Riel's trial included George Burbidge, the Deputy Attorney General, Christopher Robinson, QC, and Britton Bath Osler, QC. There were also two younger lawyers who later became the Chief Justice of Alberta and the Attorney General of Quebec. Riel could not afford defence lawyers, so supporters in Quebec provided funds. Charles Fitzpatrick, who later became the Chief Justice of Canada, led the defence. The jury was composed of six people rather than the usual twelve. All of them were English-speaking Protestants. The witnesses included General Middleton and Riel's cousin.

Regina Courthouse

A photograph of the Regina Courthouse, where Riel was tried

Riel's lawyers argued that he was mentally unstable, hoping that Riel would be acquitted by reason of insanity. He had been sent to mental asylums in Quebec twice, and it was said that a few of his writings showed signs of megalomania. Riel objected to using this defence, declaring that he did not suffer from mental health issues. Instead, he argued that his actions were justified. The prosecution argued that Riel commited high treason as the sole leader of the Rebellion. They also called in medical witnesses to refute the argument of insanity.

On the last day of Riel's trial, the two sides made their closing arguments. The defence lawyers outlined the historical role of the Métis, then argued that Riel's actions could not be a sane man's. Riel then delivered a lengthy speech defending his actions and the rights of the Métis. He dismantled his lawyers' argument for insanity, claiming that he was perfectly sane. Finally, Riel denounced the Government of Canada for its disregard for the people in the West. He hoped to be recognized as a force of good eventually. After the prosecution's summary speech, the jury found him guilty but recommended mercy. However, the judge sentenced him the death, the only possible punishment under the Treason Act. The defence attempted to appeal the verdict twice to higher courts but failed, and a medical examination of Riel's alleged insanity had an ambiguous result. The government was flooded with petitions to save Riel, but he was hung on November 16.

The last wishes of Louis Riel regarding his burial and publication of his writings

On November 27, two weeks after Riel's execution, six Cree and two Assiniboine warriors, including Wandering Spirit, were hanged at Battleford for murder. Poundmaker and Big Bear received a 3-year prison sentence. Dumont escaped to Montana but later returned after an amnesty. Riel's secretary, W. H. Jackson, was found not guilty by reason of insanity. The provisional government council received sentences ranging from conditional discharges to 7 years in prison.

Epilogue

Aftermath

The Rebellion resulted in the collapse of the provisional government, the subjugation of the Plains Indigenous peoples, and the execution of Riel. It took many decades for the Indigenous peoples and Métis of Western Canada to recover politically. The government provided land grants to the Saskatchewan Métis and resurveyed the Métis river at their wish, but they still did not have equality. Riel's trial caused upset among French Canadians, contributing to the distrust of Anglophones in Quebec that still exists today.

The matter of Riel's execution deeply divided Canadians, and would affect Canadian politics for years.

Francophones Anglophones

Although the French Canadians had supported the colonization of the West, they were outraged over Riel's execution. They interpreted the hanging of Riel as a wounding of the entire French people, an act of cruelty against a supporter of rights for French Catholics. To them, the hanging represented a Canada where French Canadians' views and feelings could be disregarded. The hanging of Riel was first in a series of events that led to the rise of Quebec separatism. Riel's execution tarnished the reputation of the Conservative Party in Quebec. It became known as "le parti des Anglais" (the party of the English). Some politicians rose to power by publicly expressing these opinions.

Conservative Ontario strongly supported Riel's execution. Some claimed that the Catholic Church encouraged the uprisings, and that if the "hot-headed semi-savages [sic] are to be made to understand that the central Government is too strong to be trifled with (and unless these lessons are learnt there can be no security for peace), then Riel must meet the punishment he has deserved twice over [...] there is no doubt whatever that he has committed at least once in his life the very crime of murder which is to be treated as unpardonable in the case of some of his late followers."

Riel's execution sparked outrage across French Canada. Six thousand protesters gathered in the streets of Montréal. A few weeks later, on November 23, an organized rally of fifty thousand protesters gathered in the Champ de Mars. Wilfred Laurier, who later became the first francophone prime minister of Canada, spoke at the rally. Some historians believe that this speech was the first step towards his rise to power. The outrage over Riel's execution also tarnished the reputation of the Conservative Party in Quebec, which led to the first majority Liberal government in Quebec in 1886.

The Rebellion had a distinct regional impact in the West. A wave of Canadian nationalism spread across western settler communities, based on the universal 'threat' of the armed Indigenous insurgency. This would help Canada reaffirm its sovereignty over the west.

The North-West Rebellion contributed to the completion of the Canadian Pacific Railway (CPR). The president of the CPR, Sir William Cornelius Van Horne, asked the government for funds in return for providing quick transportation of the Canadian troops to the west. The government accepted, and after the Rebellion was quelled, they provided the necessary funds and re-organized the CPR's debt. This allowed the railway to be completed just a few months later.

After the Rebellion, the Métis and some groups of First Nations suffered from discrimination. An era of systemic marginalization of Indigenous peoples began in Saskatchewan and Manitoba. The homes of the Métis were plundered by soldiers. Many Métis people were dispersed, and those who stayed were increasingly marginalized. The children or grandchildren of participants of the Rebellion had to pay a fee to enter Catholic schools. It wasn't until decades later that the government began to address the political, social, societal, and economic marginalization of the Métis in the west.

The Rebellion is sometimes considered a Resistance by historians. A resistance tends to be more passive than a rebellion, while a rebellion is about overthrowing. This is because the Indigenous peoples governed the land long before the Canadian government did, and the uprisings were merely reactions against Canada's control.

Many of the battlefields of the Rebellion are now National Historic Sites of Canada, such as Steele Narrows (Loon Lake), Frenchman Butte, Fish Creek, Fort Pitt, and Frog Lake.

The Rebellion has been featured in various fictional works, such as:

  • Stewart Sterling's Red Trails (1935)
  • Cecil B. DeMille's North West Mounted Police (1940)
  • Giles Lutz's The Magnificent Failure (1967)
  • Albert Silver's Lord of the Plains (1990)

Conclusion

Past-to-Present Links

Poundmaker's Exoneration

On May 23, 2019, Justin Trudeau exonerated Chief Poundmaker, stating that

It was here that Chief Poundmaker made his indelible mark on history. Here that he earned his well-deserved reputation as a diplomat and peacemaker. Here that he stood up for his people and demonstrated compassion in the face of persecution. [...] Fuelled by mistrust and a lack of understanding, government officials held Chief Poundmaker, as the recognized leader of his people, responsible for the actions of his community and convicted him of treason-felony in August 1885. [...] Today, our government acknowledges that Chief Poundmaker was a peacemaker who never stopped fighting for peace. [...] The Government of Canada recognizes that Chief Poundmaker was not a criminal, but someone who worked tirelessly to ensure the survival of his people, and hold the Crown accountable to its obligations as laid out in Treaty 6. We recognize that the unjust conviction and imprisonment of Chief Poundmaker had, and continues to have, a profound impact on the Poundmaker Cree Nation. [...] And so, as an important symbol of our desire to revitalize our relationship with the Poundmaker Cree Nation, I'm here today on behalf of the Government of Canada to confirm without reservation that Chief Poundmaker is fully exonerated of any crime or wrongdoing.

Meanwhile, the subject of Louis Riel's exoneration is one of debate. Those who argue against it claim that it will do nothing good, and that an exoneration would merely an act of political expediency.

Métis Rights

The fight for Métis rights still continues today. Métis are still not considered status Indians, and cannot claim all of the benefits that status Indians can. These benefits may include on-reserve housing, on-reserve education, and exemptions from taxes in specific situations. Recent legal cases are making advancements in Métis rights. For example, on April 14, 2016, in the case Daniels v. Canada (2016), the Supreme Court ruled that the Crown owes the same fiduciary duty to Métis as it does to Treaty Indians. Additionally, some Métis groups are fighting for the right to self-government.
